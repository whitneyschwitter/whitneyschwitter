Project Overview

The Herbivore is a compact, passive, two-in-one hydroponic-drying rack system, perfect for the home chef or urban gardener. By combining hydroponic cultivation with herb drying function, this innovative product offers users a convenient and efficient way to grow herbs indoors.

As part of a university project, I had the opportunity to participate in all aspects of the product development cycle alongside a small team. The overarching goal of this project was to create a tool or product to cultivate a plant of the group’s choosing. Our initial plant choice was basil , but the functionality of our product extends to a multitude of different herbs including chives, cilantro, dill, mint, oregano, parsley, rosemary, thyme, and watercress.

Project Type: University project, team project

Skills: Product design, UX design, UX research, Illustrator, computer-aided design, 3D printing




Ideation & Conceptualization

Recognizing the problem & identifying market opportunity


Quickly into the research phase, we discovered that while the average recipe calls for around one quarter ounce of herbs, most packages available for purchase contain around eight times that. This realization led our team to pivot towards developing a product that could help prevent food waste.


Key Takeaways From Market Research

  • Rising cost in food prices nationwide
  • Emerging consumer trend of a “green revolution”
  • Covid-19 drastically altered how people cook and eat at home, including availability of ingredients and ability to dine out

Quantitative Insights Derived from Surveys

Taken from a sample survey of 200 people ages 18 to 30


  • 29% of respondents said they had never finished a pack of herbs
  • 57% of surveyees frequently found themselves wasting herbs
  • 33% made a conscious effort to avoid food waste
  • 71% had negative feelings about wasting food
  • 74% felt concerned about rising prices of groceries
  • 20% expressed interest in growing their own food
  • 61% noted they would be open to growing their own food if it required minimal effort


Concept Sketches


The initial idea we came up with was an expandable and retractable ring that could fit around any planter and double as an herb drying rack. However, we found that herbs thrive in dark environments during the drying process.



A major design constraint we had to consider were the materials used to construct the working prototypes. Although our material of choice was recycled wood from the wood shop, time constraints and the decision to create a circular planter influenced us to 3D print the models using PLA.

The decision to stack the drying rack minimizes the amount of space taken up, making the product perfect for dorm rooms and smaller apartments in line with the lifestyles of our intended audience.

Combining a passive hydroponic planter with a herb drying function emerged as a unique and desirable concept, meeting the requirements for space efficiency and preservation of herb qualities, perfect for an urban home cook or college student.

Prototyping

Multiple iterations of prototypes were constructed to refine the design and functionality.

The hydroponic system was carefully calibrated to maintain ideal nutrient levels, pH balance, and water circulation.

The drying function was optimized to ensure efficient airflow and temperature control, preserving the herbs' flavor. Our initial design included an enclosed drying rack, but after further interactions we decided to implement a lattice pattern that was congruent with the logo.



Final Prototype



After a few failed prints and countless hours in the engineering lab spent removing PLA from the exterior lattice design, we had a functioning prototype.

Through a systematic and iterative approach, our team delivered a solution that met the needs of urban gardeners and young home cooks.


Why Herbivore?


  • Compact dimensions make it suitable for small spaces like window sills in apartments or dorm rooms.

  • Research shows the Kratky method yields 30 to 50 percent more growth than a traditional soil planter, plus no soil means less mess.

  • The Herbivore utilizes passive hydroponics so users only have to water their plants once every harvesting cycle.

  • No need to keep track of when to water, convenient water-level indicator allows the user to keep track of when the plant needs to be watered again.

  • This 2-in-1 system has everything you need, so no need to purchase an additional drying rack.


Pain Points & Moving Forward


  • Material & final presentation: design constraints restricted us from constructing a final prototype out of the intended material, recycled wood.

  • Space functionality trade-off: we had to compromise on a size that was big enough to preserve functionality, but not take up too much space. In further iterations, we could consider different scales or even a modular system.

  • Maintenance: to simplify user interaction, we included a floating water-level monitor to keep users from continuously opening the planter, which could affect the growth rate. Still remembering to clean the planter once every harvesting cycle might pose a slight challenge to the user.
